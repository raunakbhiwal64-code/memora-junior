1. Executive summary
Most children are taught what to remember but never how to remember. They re-read and cram, which produces fragile, short-lived recall. The memory palace (method of loci) is one of the oldest and most reliable techniques for durable recall, but it is almost never taught to kids because the existing material is abstract, adult-oriented, and assumes a level of self-directed imagination that younger learners need scaffolding to reach.
This product turns the memory palace into a guided, visual, gamified experience built for children. Instead of asking a child to imagine an empty room, the app gives them ready-made, illustrated palaces (a treehouse, an aquarium, a space station), walks them step-by-step through placing what they want to remember, and then uses spaced practice to lock it in. The goal is not a single party trick but a transferable skill the child can apply to spellings, times tables, science facts, vocabulary, and beyond.
The first release is deliberately small: a web-only, no-account validation build that proves children can learn the technique and that it improves recall in a measurable way. Everything past that — accounts, parent dashboards, curriculum packs, classroom features — is sequenced behind that proof.

2. Problem statement
Three problems sit underneath this product:
Memorisation is taught by repetition, not by technique. Children rely on rote re-reading and cramming, which the learning-science literature consistently shows produces poor long-term retention compared with elaborative and spatial strategies.
Powerful techniques exist but are inaccessible to kids. Method of loci, the peg system, and story-chaining all work, but the available instruction is text-heavy, adult-framed, and gives no visual scaffold — so children can't form the vivid mental images the technique depends on.
Parents and teachers lack a trustworthy, screen-time-justifiable tool. Existing memory apps are either dry flashcard drills or attention-farming games. There is a gap for something that is calm, demonstrably educational, and safe for children by design.
If we solve these, the payoff is a child who carries a real, reusable cognitive skill — and a parent or teacher who can see the recall improvement in numbers.

3. Vision & product strategy
3.1 Vision
Every child learns how to remember — as a skill they own, not a chore they endure.
3.2 Strategic pillars
Technique over content. We are teaching a transferable method. Content (spellings, facts, lists) is the vehicle; the durable skill is the product.
Guided, not blank-canvas. Children get pre-built palaces and step-by-step coaching. We remove the imagination cold-start that makes loci hard for kids and beginners.
Calm by design. No streak anxiety, no dark-pattern engagement loops. Reward comes from visible mastery and recall, which parents and teachers can endorse.
Evidence-led. Recall improvement is measured and shown. The product earns trust by proving it works, not by claiming it.
From learning to real use. The guided palaces teach the skill; the child's own photographed palaces let them use it for real, at scale. The product should carry a child from first lesson to hundreds of items stored in places they actually know.

4. Goals & non-goals
4.1 Goals (v1)
Teach a child the core memory-palace loop — choose a palace, place items, walk it, recall — in a single 10-minute first session.
Demonstrate a measurable recall lift versus rote memorisation on a controlled list (e.g. a 10–15 item set).
Make the experience genuinely enjoyable and repeatable for an 8–12 year old without an adult sitting beside them.
Validate demand and learning efficacy with a small cohort before investing in accounts, payments, or classroom tooling.
Product vision beyond v1: make the product genuinely practical — a child can build their own palaces from photos of places they know and reliably store and retain hundreds of items across several saved palaces. v1 proves the technique works; the capacity story is what makes it worth keeping.
4.2 Non-goals (explicitly out of scope for v1)
User accounts, logins, and saved profiles (deferred to Phase 2).
Payments, subscriptions, or any monetisation.
Native iOS / Android apps.
Teacher / classroom dashboards and multi-student management.
Build-your-own photo palaces and multiple saved palaces — a core part of the product vision, but deferred to Phase 2/3 because they require accounts, storage, and a photo-safety pipeline (see Section 7).

6. The core concept — a memory palace built for kids
An adult memory palace asks you to recall a familiar place and mentally walk through it, attaching vivid, exaggerated images to fixed locations (“loci”). Kids can do this, but two things break the experience: forming a blank mental space is hard, and the imagery instruction is abstract. We fix both by making the palace concrete and on-screen.
6.1 The core loop
Pick a palace. The child chooses an illustrated environment they love — Treehouse, Aquarium, Space Station, Pirate Ship — each with a fixed set of clearly numbered “spots.”
Get the list. The thing to remember (a curated demo list in v1, e.g. planets, a shopping list, spelling words) is shown one item at a time.
Place & picture. For each spot, the app prompts the child to imagine a silly, exaggerated picture linking the item to that spot, and reinforces it with on-screen art and a short audio cue.
Walk the palace. The child takes a guided “walk” past each spot in order, rehearsing the images.
Recall & reveal. The app hides the items and asks the child to recall them in order, then scores and celebrates the result.
Come back later. Spaced practice prompts the child to revisit the same palace after a delay to prove the memory stuck.
6.2 Why this works for children specifically
The spatial scaffold is provided, so there is no blank-canvas cold start.
Exaggerated, funny imagery is naturally appealing to kids and is exactly what the technique requires.
Numbered spots give an unambiguous order, which is the hardest part of loci to self-manage.
Short, contained sessions match a child's attention span and produce a quick win.

7. Custom photo palaces & storing at scale
The guided palaces in Section 6 teach the technique. To make the product practical — to hold hundreds of items — a child needs to turn places they actually know into palaces. A real, familiar home is the single strongest memory palace there is, because the spatial memory already exists and costs nothing to recall. This section covers building your own palace from photos, keeping many of them, and retaining everything stored across them.
7.1 Build-your-own from photos
Capture a route. Walk a fixed path through a real space — home, classroom, a grandparent's house — and photograph distinct spots in order.
Turn photos into loci. The app arranges the photos into a numbered path, so each photo becomes an ordered “spot” on the walk.
Place items on real spots. The child attaches what they want to remember to those personally-meaningful locations — which encode far better than generic art, because the space is already deeply familiar.
7.2 Why this unlocks scale (the capacity math)
Custom palaces are how the “hundreds of items” goal is actually met. The arithmetic is simple:
A typical home has roughly 8–10 rooms, each with 8–12 strong, distinct spots — about 80–120 items in a single palace.
A small library of palaces (home, school, grandparents', the walk to the park) reaches several hundred items comfortably.
This is exactly how memory athletes store large volumes; the product's job is to make the setup easy and the upkeep automatic.
7.3 Multiple saved palaces (a palace library)
A personal library. Each child keeps their own set of palaces, each nameable — “Home,” “Spanish words,” “History timeline.”
Theme by subject. Putting different bodies of knowledge in different spaces aids recall by reducing interference between unrelated lists.
Why it forces accounts forward. Saving and reopening palaces across sessions needs persistent, private per-child storage — which is the main reason the account work moves into Phase 2.
7.4 Keeping hundreds of items learned (review at scale)
Capacity is worthless if items don't stay learned. Once a child is holding hundreds of items, spaced repetition stops being optional and becomes the backbone of the product.
A review queue. A scheduler surfaces which spots, in which palaces, are due for review today — so the child reviews a manageable handful rather than everything at once.
Per-item scheduling. Items the child recalls easily are spaced out further; ones they miss come back sooner.
7.5 Critical constraints — photographing a child's real home
This is the highest-sensitivity feature in the product. Photographing where a child lives is not something to treat casually. The following are constraints, not options:
Parent-gated. Creating or uploading a custom palace requires a verified parent/guardian. Realistically, the parent performs the capture; the child does the placing and recalling.
Strip metadata on import. Remove EXIF and geolocation from every photo as it is added, so a saved palace can never leak where the child lives.
Private by construction. Photos belong to that child only — never shared, never public, never used to train any model — with one-tap delete of an entire palace.
Places, not people. Guidance (and ideally automatic detection) to keep faces and people out of palace photos; loci should be objects and locations.
Prefer on-device. Process and, where feasible, store photos on the device; if any cloud storage is used, encrypt it and minimise what is kept.
These constraints are exactly why this is a Phase 2/3 capability and not a v1 one: it cannot ship until the account, parental-consent, and secure-storage foundations exist.

The product is built on a small number of well-established memory principles. Naming them here keeps feature decisions honest and gives the parent/teacher story its credibility.

9.3 Acceptance criteria (sample — core loop)
A child can complete pick → place → walk → recall for a 10-item list in under 10 minutes without adult help.
The recall step requires the child to produce items (typed or spoken/selected from a shuffled pool), not merely recognise a single answer.
The result screen never uses red “wrong” framing; misses are shown as “let's try that spot again.”
The full flow works on a tablet in landscape and on a laptop, with touch and mouse.

11. UX principles for children
One thing on screen at a time. Each step is a single, obvious action. No dense menus or settings in the child's path.
Show, don't tell. Demonstrate the imagery with art rather than explaining it in paragraphs a child won't read.
Big targets, forgiving input. Large tap areas, generous spacing, no precision required; works with imperfect typing.
Encouraging, never punishing. Warm feedback for every attempt; mistakes are framed as another go, not failure.
Calm, not addictive. No streaks-as-pressure, no loot mechanics, no endless feed. Sessions have a clear finish.
Accessible by default. Audio narration, high-contrast text, dyslexia-friendly type option, keyboard and touch support.

12. Content scope (v1)
Three to four curated palaces and a small set of demo lists are enough to validate. Curating tightly also avoids moderation and quality risk that user-generated content would introduce.
Palaces: Treehouse, Aquarium, Space Station, Pirate Ship — each with consistently numbered spots and a distinct illustration set.
Demo lists: the eight planets, a 10-animal list, and an age-appropriate spelling set — chosen to make a rote-vs-palace comparison easy to demonstrate.
Tone of imagery prompts: playful and exaggerated, always age-appropriate; no scary, violent, or otherwise unsuitable imagery.
Relationship to custom palaces: the curated set is the on-ramp — it teaches the technique with zero setup and zero data. The build-your-own photo palaces of Section 7 are the graduation path that arrives in Phase 2, once a child has learned the method on the guided palaces.

14. Child safety, privacy & compliance
Because the audience is children, this section is a first-class requirement, not an afterthought. The v1 architecture is chosen partly to minimise obligations here.
Collect nothing in v1. No accounts, no names, no emails, no persistent identifiers. If any analytics are used, they must be anonymous and aggregate-only, with no behavioural profiling of children.
Plan for COPPA / GDPR-K before Phase 2. The moment accounts or any personal data enter the picture, verifiable parental consent, data-minimisation, and clear retention/deletion rules become mandatory. Design the consent flow before, not after, building accounts.
No third-party ad tracking, ever. No advertising SDKs, no behavioural ad networks, no data sale. This is both an ethical line and a trust differentiator with parents.
No social or open-input exposure. No child-to-child contact and no public free-text that could surface unmoderated content to a child.
Content safety. All imagery and prompts curated and reviewed for age-appropriateness; no scary or distressing content.
Home photos are the highest-risk surface (Phase 2). The build-your-own palace feature means photographing where a child lives. It must be parent-gated, must strip EXIF/geolocation on import, must keep photos private and never shared or used for training, must steer faces and people out of shots, and must allow one-tap deletion of a whole palace. Prefer on-device processing and storage; encrypt and minimise anything that touches a server. Full detail in Section 7.5.
Get a real legal review. Treat the above as product intent, not legal advice — have the consent model and privacy policy reviewed by someone qualified in the markets you launch in.
